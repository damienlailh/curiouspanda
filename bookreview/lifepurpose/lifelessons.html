Life Lessons

When I look back at the past 19 years of my life, I can’t help but marvel at the eclectic range of experiences and the many lessons I have learnt from them. But with every new adventure, my mind naturally wanders but to the perennial question that has mystified humans for centuries--what are we here for? What is the meaning of life? In hopes of attaining a glimpse of the answer, I got my hands on a few books that have had huge inspirations for me. Today, I would like to share some of my takeaways with you and inspire you to embark on a similar journey.

Finding meaning amidst despair--Man’s Search For Meaning

This book was personally quite profound as the setting in which the author wrote this book, in a Nazi camp where his freedom was curtailed, had an uncanny similarity to my current situation serving the military under a conscription service (thankfully a lot better than what Frankl described). Specifically, Frankl believed that the deepest motivation for humans to live is meaning and purpose--this is why some prisoners who lose hope die while some catch a glimpse of meaning amidst the suffering. Throughout our lives, we may inevitably be forced against our will, to do things that we dislike and for our freedom to be restricted. If these prisoners could still find purpose in such grotesque conditions, why can’t we? As Frankl puts it, “Everything can be taken from a person except one thing: the freedom to choose one’s attitude”. Furthermore, Frankl expresses his own opinions on the perennial question, “What is the meaning of life?”. For Frankl, he believes that it is not us who ask this question to life, but rather life questioning us as we navigate the vicissitudes of life. In other words, there is no universal meaning of life that perpetuates throughout your lifetime--depending on your current circumstances and past experiences, what is purposeful and fulfilling would vary. I believe this is a powerful way of navigating life--if the premise that humans are fueled by meaning and purpose in our lives, then being able to find fulfillment in an eclectic range of situations helps to build resilience and adaptability. Overall, Frankl’s story and his concept of logotherapy are undoubtedly valuable lessons that are worth everyone’s time to ponder over.

Disney Storyline with You as the Main Character--The Alchemist

“When you want something, all the universe conspires in helping you to achieve it.” This alone should give you a hint that this story may be right out of a childhood disney movie--it is, but somehow, it works. At its core, The Alchemist argues that everyone has a personal legend, something that we are born to do, yet many lose sight of it as we focus on staying afloat amidst the waves of life. But sometimes, we have to “follow the omens” and trust your intuition--do you long for something? Is there something you have always wanted to do but are afraid of the drastic change? If so, perhaps that is your calling. Santiago himself had had to sell his sheep, gotten robbed, and leave the love of his life to seek out his personal legend. We humans always desire the best of both worlds--to maintain our stable life while setting out on the adventure we also yearned for. To that, The Alchemist tells us that “the fear of failure is worse than failing itself”. Sometimes, we just have to “follow the omens” and take the necessary leap of faith. While alchemy is concerned with transforming lead to gold, the true change is within oneself as he seeks out his personal legend. Ultimately, the storyline seems cliche and overused, but the motivation provided is powerful nonetheless--I still recall the galvanising impact The Alchemist had on me when I opened it for the first time. While it is unlikely that I will read it over and over again, I believe that everyone should read it once in their life.

Having the Courage to Fulfil Your Personal Legend--The Big Leap

There are 2 key concepts mentioned in the book--the Upper Limit Problem and the Zone of Genius. The Upper Limit Problem refers to the natural tendency for ourselves to self-destruct or self-sabotage when we are experiencing success. There were 4 reasons given, but I think it really boils down to the lack of self-belief and confidence that you deserve this level of success. Recalling my own experiences, it is truly hard to argue otherwise. I have had my fair share of the Upper Limit Problem--I recall myself refusing to try out certain new learning opportunities that were offered to me as I did not believe I could be capable of conquering them. For instance, in my early Secondary School days, I was given the chance to participate in heavy-commitment research projects and truthfully, I was excited to be part of it. However, I was afraid that my ability in research would pale in comparison to my inclinations towards the Olympiads. In the end, I ended up crawling back to my comfort zone where I was more familiar with and giving up on these learning opportunities. Yet, after all these years, I realised something--I really enjoyed learning, getting challenged, and helping others to do the same. Unfortunately, my Upper Limit Problem prevented me from reaching my Zone of Genius. What this book really emphasises is the courage to take “The Big Leap”. It’s about the guts to believe that you can be meant for greatness, you can reach the Zone of Genius that God has prepared for you, and you can fulfil every last ounce of potential in you. The Big Leap is not only an inspirational book, but it is a pragmatic one that forces you to reflect on your own fears and shortcomings, be fully transparent with yourself, and commit to taking action to change the reality. I believe this book is an amazing wake-up call for many of us, and even more so for “high-achievers” like you.

You are NOT What you Do--The New Earth by Eckhart Tolle

It is not surprising when you hear athletes, who have pursued perfection in one narrow field, feeling lost in life. For the most part of their lives, they have identified themselves with the sport to the extent that they could not even imagine life in the absence of it. In The New Earth, Tolle describes this insatiable desire to identify with something tangible as the workings of the Ego. The Ego clouds you from reaching your true self--the higher level consciousness that is the true essence of you. In a sense, this is very similar to spiritual teachings of religions such as Buddhism. Admittedly, this is quite a complex book to start with but it is an extremely intriguing read. Personally, my biggest takeaway is about seeking peace--avoid the immense fluctuations in dopamine and emotions that today’s environment encourages. Look internally for tranquility and avoid associating your true value with any external factors. If we can do so, we will be less dependent on external influences for our self-fulfilment.

Conclusion

For these trailblazers, their understandings of the meaning of life vary significantly. But who are we to say if anyone is correct or wrong? Perhaps the true meaning of life lies in their pursuit of this truth. As a wise man once said, “it is not the pursuit of happiness, but the happiness of the pursuit”. May you all find your Personal Legends.
