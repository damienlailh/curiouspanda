Structure of a GP Essay

This is probably the segment where students are the most eager to learn about as it gives them concrete assurance in such an amorphous field. Before I continue, I would just like to highlight that GP is extremely flexible, so what has worked for me may differ from what your teachers recommend. It is then important that you think critically about all the resources you are exposed to and make the decision for yourself.

Introduction

In the beginning of your essay, there are two main parts that catch the marker’s attention--your hook and thesis. The hook is basically an interesting way to tell your marker “this is the topic that we will be discussing today”. If you recall my sample essay, I started off with “Rising global temperatures, mass extinctions of biodiversity, and rising sea levels…”. Before anyone reads on, the topic that immediately comes to mind is--this student is writing something pertinent to climate change or environment sustainability. As such, please refrain from writing overtly obscure hooks or oblique references. The purpose of the hook is to pique the reader’s interest about the topic you are about to introduce them to, and not to confuse them. Next, the thesis unequivocally states your stance and tells your reader what you are about to argue for. If there is one lesson you can take away for the introduction, it is to BE CLEAR! Some may be tempted to impress the markers through various means, but more often than not, clarity is the most important criteria for your introduction. After all, you are setting the stage for your arguments--if they do not even know what you are arguing for, I do not reckon that your arguments would be very much appreciated.

1 CA + 2SA vs 1CA + 3SA

For the content paragraphs, one debate I would like to address is the number of paragraphs we should write. Most teachers would advocate for one counterargument (CA) and three supporting arguments (SA). However, some students may face one of two problems--either they have insufficient time to finish everything or they do not have 3 extremely convincing arguments to support their stance. In that light, my suggestion is always this: quality > quantity. If you have two really cogent arguments that you wish to fully develop, why not stick to just two supporting arguments? Ultimately, the Cambridge markers are not there to count the number of paragraphs you have written, but rather they are interested in the quality of your argumentation. As such, having lesser but more well-developed points may in fact benefit you. In fact, for my A-levels, I only wrote 2SAs and I got an A! My advice? Start with 1CA + 2SA--if you find that you struggle to sufficiently develop your points with just two SAs, then adopt the three SAs approach. Ultimately, you can discuss your work with teachers or friends to slowly adjust your style. This takes time so trust the process.

Content Paragraph

PEEL, SEXI, and so on…you have probably heard numerous acronyms for the structure of your content paragraph. They are essentially just a skeleton for how you can develop your arguments.

Topic Sentence

This is answering the question: Why do you agree/disagree with the question posed? That’s it! Don’t overcomplicate it! Before you attempt to add more complexity to your topic sentences, always remember that you must answer the question and be lucid. As you journey deeper into your GP journey, you may start to develop your style and add in point-of-views such as “sceptics may argue…”. For now, ensure your fundamentals are rock-solid and always communicate clearly.

Elaboration, Examples & Explanation

This is where your argument development occurs and why I believe 2SAs is more than sufficient--if you fully expound on your 3Es, I am very certain you will easily write almost one page for each paragraph! In this exact sequence, allow me to walk you through the importance of each step.

Firstly, elaborate--what is the topic sentence saying? This is a short few sentences which you use to give readers greater details as to the reason for why you are supporting/disagreeing. I like to have 1-2 sentences of elaboration before diving into my examples. Next, examples--this is arguably the most important yet easiest component of the argument. Without examples, is it difficult for you to go beyond a ‘B’ grade in GP so please read widely and gather an eclectic range of examples if required. Examples help to concretise your arguments by ensuring that your reasoning applies to the real-world and not solely in your imagination. However, not all examples are equal--it is crucial to judiciously select salient examples that are both significant and prominent for them to truly pack a punch.

Last but not least, explanation is what differentiates you from the rest of the cohort--most people believe it is merely linking your examples to your topic sentence. Big, big mistake! Remember the 20% unique insights I mentioned in the “Introduction to General Paper”? Explanation is where you inject different angles of analysis to the same few examples and impress the examiners with your understanding of the topic. For more information, please take a look at my article on “Nuancing and Evaluation”.

Conclusion

The conclusion serves two purposes--bring the reader back to the overarching reasons of the discussion, and perhaps raise 1-2 intriguing insights you yourself have obtained from this learning experience. I have faith that the former is obvious to you, so I will focus on the latter. For instance, if we consider my sample essay, I have argued extensively on why environmental conservation is futile AT THE MOMENT. However, are there steps that humanity can take to change this fact? Ultimately, the conclusion is where you can add your own personal touch so feel free to explore!
