Introduction to General Paper

For everyone taking A levels, general paper (GP) is most likely the subject that they are the most worried about. Specifically, the essay is unsettling--seemingly no way to systematically study for it yet having one of the largest impacts on your University Admission Score. Today, I will share some insights I have learnt over my A-level journey. For some context, I am a RI alumni who have consistently done well in GP (A grade since Year 5). I have also attained the top in English Language and Higher Mother Tongue Bilingual Prize in Year 4. Here is my small take on how to tackle the essay.

The Big Picture

Firstly, we need to understand the big picture that most people skip yet it is arguably the most crucial aspect to kickstart your thinking. Ask yourself this one question--what is the purpose of writing a GP essay? Did Cambridge or SEAB randomly scroll about wikipedia and decide to ask a question about something they read? (I might do that so thank god I’m not a GP teacher). If we look at the past questions, it is increasingly evident that their questions concern perennial issues--in other words, controversial issues where debates have been raging for years without any consensus. You have probably heard people saying, “Read the news! Stay informed!” But, why??? The type of questions they ask involve current affairs that shape the type of conversations and debate that concern the entire world. This is what Cambridge is interested in finding out from you! What are your opinions on some of the most important yet unresolved issues in the world?

The Biggest Myth on Originality

By extension of the previous paragraph, these widely debated issues would naturally have a wealth of perspectives and information surrounding it. This means that unless you are William Shakespeare, no marker expects you to reinvent the wheel, devise innovative points the world has never thought about before, and make your case for them. I know some individuals believe that in order to stand out, they must make points different from everyone else--NO! You score brownie points by making cogent arguments rather than oblique ones that no one explores. As such, when you are starting out in your journey into GP, read widely about what others have to say and understand their arguments. For instance, when I first read about the A-level question on social media, I realised I found an Oxford debate on the exact same question! Listen to what they have to say! Because more often than not, they will present you points that are both relevant and well supported by current evidence, allowing you to adapt the points for easy usage. After you have mastered your basics, you may like to add some spice to the mix by infusing your unique ideas and perspectives. Regarding this, I like to adhere to the Pareto Principle--80% of your paragraph should adhere to a well-justified and well-known idea combined with 20% of your own unique insights. Doing so will ensure that you will score a minimally satisfactory grade.
